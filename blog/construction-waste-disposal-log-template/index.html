Why You Need a Waste Disposal Log

If you're in construction, waste disposal records aren't optional—they're legally required for EPA and state environmental compliance. Whether you're a solo contractor or a 50-person company, if you generate waste (especially hazardous materials like lead paint, asbestos, or contaminated soil), you need documentation.

The stakes:

  • EPA audits: Lack of records = presumption of improper disposal
  • Fines: RCRA violations carry substantial federal civil penalties (varying by violation type under 40 CFR § 19.4)
  • Liability protection: Disposal logs prove you followed the law
  • Retention requirement: Records must be kept for 3-5 years
Good news: Basic waste tracking is simple. This article provides a free template and step-by-step guide to filling it out correctly.

What Information Legally Must Be Tracked

Minimum Required Information (EPA Compliance)

For all waste disposal, you should track:

  1. Date: When waste was removed from the site
  2. Project/Location: Job site address where waste originated
  3. Waste Type: General description (e.g., "construction debris", "lead paint waste", "asbestos floor tiles")
  4. Quantity: Volume (cubic yards) or weight (tons/pounds)
  5. Hauler: Company name and license/permit number
  6. Manifest Number: Unique tracking number (for hazardous waste)
  7. Disposal Facility: Name and location of final disposal site

Additional Information for Hazardous Waste

If you're disposing of RCRA-regulated hazardous waste (lead, asbestos, contaminated soil), you must also track:

  • EPA Waste Code: (e.g., D008 for lead, K048 for petroleum-contaminated soil)
  • Generator Status: VSQG, SQG, or LQG (see RCRA recordkeeping guide)
  • Manifest Signature Date: When hauler signed the manifest
  • Disposal Confirmation Date: When facility confirmed receipt
Important: For hazardous waste, you must receive a signed copy of the manifest from the disposal facility within 60 days. If you don't, you must file an "exception report" with the EPA.

Step-by-Step Guide: How to Fill Out Logs Correctly

Column 1: Date

What to record: Date waste was removed from the site (not when it was generated)

Format: MM/DD/YYYY (e.g., 10/15/2025)

Why it matters: EPA uses dates to verify compliance with storage time limits (180 days for Small Quantity Generators, 90 days for Large Quantity Generators)

Column 2: Project Name / Address

What to record: Job site name and full address

Example: "123 Oak Street Remodel, Portland, OR 97201"

Why it matters: Links waste to specific projects for liability tracking. If a site is later found to have contamination, you can prove you properly disposed of waste from that location.

Column 3: Waste Type

What to record: Specific description of waste

Examples:

  • "Construction debris (wood, drywall, metal)"
  • "Lead paint debris (pre-1978 building)"
  • "Asbestos floor tiles (9x9 inch, vinyl)"
  • "Contaminated soil (petroleum)"

Why it matters: Determines if waste is hazardous and what disposal regulations apply. Be specific—"construction waste" is too vague; "lead paint debris" triggers hazardous waste protocols.

Column 4: Quantity

What to record: Volume or weight of waste

Format: Cubic yards (for bulk debris) or pounds/tons (for hazardous waste)

Examples:

  • "30 cubic yards" (dumpster size)
  • "250 pounds" (lead paint debris)
  • "1.5 tons" (asbestos materials)

Why it matters: Quantity determines generator status (VSQG vs SQG vs LQG) and impacts disposal costs.

Column 5: Hauler Name

What to record: Full legal name of waste hauling company

Example: "ABC Waste Services, Inc."

Why it matters: Proves you used a licensed hauler (EPA requirement). If waste is illegally dumped, hauler records prove you hired a legitimate company.

Column 6: Hauler License #

What to record: State waste hauler permit/license number

Example: "OR-12345" (Oregon waste hauler license)

Why it matters: Verifies hauler is legally authorized to transport waste. EPA will check this during audits.

How to find it: Ask hauler for their permit number, or check your state environmental agency's database of licensed haulers.

Column 7: Manifest # (if hazardous)

What to record: Unique manifest tracking number from EPA Form 8700-22

Example: "HW-2025-9876"

When required: Only for hazardous waste (lead, asbestos, contaminated soil, etc.)

Why it matters: Manifest is the legal "chain of custody" document. EPA can trace waste from your site to final disposal using this number.

Pro tip: Write "N/A" for non-hazardous waste. Don't leave blank—blanks suggest incomplete records.

Column 8: Disposal Facility

What to record: Name and location of final disposal site

Example: "Clean Harbors Portland, 1234 Industrial Way, Portland OR"

Why it matters: Proves waste went to a properly permitted facility (not illegally dumped). EPA will verify facility is authorized to accept that type of waste.

Column 9: Notes

What to record: Any additional relevant information

Examples:

  • "Manifest signed 10/16, confirmed by facility 10/18"
  • "Photo documentation attached (see Project Binder)"
  • "Lab test confirmed lead content >5000 ppm"
  • "Exception report filed—manifest not returned within 60 days"

Common Mistakes That Trigger EPA Audits

1. Vague Waste Descriptions

Wrong: "Construction waste"
Right: "Wood framing, drywall, metal studs—no hazardous materials"

Why it matters: Auditors assume "construction waste" might include hazardous materials. Be specific to avoid scrutiny.

2. Missing Quantities

Wrong: Leaving quantity column blank or writing "1 dumpster"
Right: "30 cubic yards (1 dumpster)" or "250 pounds (5 drums)"

Why it matters: Quantity determines generator category. Missing data = presumption of non-compliance.

3. No Hauler License Numbers

Wrong: Only recording hauler name
Right: Hauler name + state license/permit number

Why it matters: Unlicensed haulers often illegally dump waste. EPA will verify all haulers are licensed—missing numbers = red flag.

4. Incomplete Hazardous Waste Records

Wrong: Recording lead paint disposal without manifest number or disposal facility
Right: Full manifest number, facility name/location, confirmation date

Why it matters: Hazardous waste violations carry criminal penalties. Incomplete records = presumption of illegal disposal.

5. Not Keeping Records Long Enough

Wrong: Throwing out logs after 1 year
Right: Keeping records for 3-5 years (depending on generator status)

Why it matters: EPA audits can go back 3-5 years. Missing historical records = fines for every missing manifest.

Photo Documentation Best Practices

In addition to written logs, photos are powerful evidence during EPA audits. Take photos of:

What to Photograph

  • Waste containers on-site: Shows type and quantity of waste
  • Hauler trucks: Captures company name, license plate, truck number
  • Manifests before signing: Backup in case hauler loses paperwork
  • Waste labels: Proves proper labeling (e.g., "Hazardous Waste—Lead Paint Debris")
  • Before/after disposal: Shows waste was removed from site

How to Store Photos

  • File naming: "2025-10-15_OakStreet_LeadPaintWaste_Manifest.jpg"
  • Storage: Attach to disposal log (if digital) or print and include in project binder
  • Retention: Keep photos for same period as written logs (3-5 years)
Pro tip: Use your phone's timestamp feature. Photos with date/time metadata are stronger evidence than handwritten notes.

Where to Store Records

Physical copies:

  • Store in fireproof file cabinet (if possible)
  • Organize by project or year
  • Keep at business office (not in truck or job site trailer)

Digital copies:

  • Scan all manifests and logs
  • Store in cloud backup (Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive)
  • Keep backups in multiple locations
Warning: Storing records only on a job site trailer or truck is risky. If they're stolen or lost in a fire, you have no backup—and EPA will still expect records during an audit.

EPA Audit Preparation: What Auditors Expect to See

When EPA conducts a waste compliance audit, they'll request:

  1. All manifests for the audit period (typically 3-5 years)
  2. Proof of disposal facility permits (confirms facilities were authorized to accept waste)
  3. Hauler license verification (shows you used licensed haulers)
  4. Disposal logs showing dates, quantities, waste types
  5. Exception reports (if any manifests weren't returned)

How to Prepare for an Audit (Even If You Haven't Been Notified)

Quarterly audit readiness drill (2 hours):

  • Pull all manifests from the last 3 months
  • Verify every manifest has a corresponding log entry
  • Check that disposal facilities are still EPA-permitted (look them up on state database)
  • File missing manifests into project binders

If you can generate a complete waste disposal report for the past 3 years in under 30 minutes, you're audit-ready.
