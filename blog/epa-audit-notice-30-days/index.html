EPA Audit Notice: What Construction Companies Need to Do in 30 Days

Just received an EPA audit notice? Here's your 30-day action plan with crisis-mode guidance, record reconstruction strategies, and exactly what auditors expect to see.

Opening the Letter: What Just Happened?

You just received an official notice from the EPA (or your state environmental agency) informing you that your construction company is being audited for waste disposal compliance. Your heart sinks. You have 30 days to respond with documentation proving proper waste handling.

This is not the time to panic.

This is the time to act methodically.

EPA audits of construction companies are more common than most contractors realize. Between 2023-2024, the EPA conducted over 2,400 compliance audits in the construction sector alone. If you're reading this, you're not alone—and more importantly, you can get through this.

This guide walks you through exactly what to do in the next 30 days.

Day 1-3: Understand What They're Asking For

Read the Notice Carefully

EPA audit notices typically request documentation for:

  1. Waste manifests - Proof of where hazardous waste went
  2. Hauler receipts - Evidence of who transported waste
  3. Disposal site records - Confirmation of final disposal location
  4. Quantity logs - How much waste was generated and disposed
  5. Project timelines - Dates of waste generation and removal
ACTION: Highlight every specific request in the notice. Create a checklist of what you need to provide.

Identify the Audit Scope

Most EPA audits focus on:

  • Time period: Often the last 12-36 months
  • Specific projects: Job sites mentioned in complaints or inspections
  • Waste types: Hazardous materials (asbestos, lead paint, contaminated soil) or large-volume debris
ACTION: Note the exact timeframe and projects mentioned. You'll need records for ONLY this scope—don't overwhelm yourself trying to document everything you've ever done.

Understand the Stakes

EPA fines for construction waste violations can be severe:

  • Base fine: RCRA violations carry substantial federal civil penalties (varying by violation type under 40 CFR § 19.4)
  • Criminal penalties: Possible for knowing violations
  • Project delays: Stop-work orders if violations are ongoing
ACTION: Accept the seriousness, but stay focused. Most audits are resolved with documentation and minor penalties—catastrophic fines only happen with egregious or willful violations.

Professional site inspections and documentation are critical during EPA audits

Day 4-7: Gather What You Already Have

Collect Existing Records

Start with what's easy to find:

Digital Records (30 minutes):

  • Email searches: "invoice", "waste", "disposal", "hauler", "manifest"
  • Accounting software: Filter for waste-related expenses
  • Project management tools: Look for disposal notes or photos

Paper Records (2-3 hours):

  • File cabinets: Pull all project folders for the audit period
  • Invoice binders: Find waste hauler bills
  • Site photos: Check for timestamped images of waste containers

Hauler Records (1-2 hours):

  • Call your regular waste companies and request copies of all manifests/receipts
  • Most haulers keep records for 3+ years and can email PDFs within days
ACTION: Create a physical folder (or digital folder) labeled "EPA Audit - [Your Company] - [Date]". Put everything related to the audit here. Organization now saves hours later.

Identify the Gaps

After 3-4 days of gathering, you'll see patterns:

  • ✅ What you have: Invoices from your primary hauler for 80% of jobs
  • ❌ What you're missing: Manifests for hazardous waste removal, disposal site confirmations, exact quantities
ACTION: Make a list of missing records. Prioritize the audit's specific requests—if they didn't ask for it, you don't need to provide it (but keep it for your files anyway).

Day 8-15: Reconstruct Missing Records

The Reality: You Won't Have Everything

Here's the truth: Most small and mid-sized construction companies don't have perfect waste records. The EPA knows this. What they want to see is:
  1. Good-faith effort to maintain records
  2. Reasonable reconstruction where records are missing
  3. Honesty about what you don't know

How to Reconstruct Records

Method 1: Invoice-Based Reconstruction

Even without manifests, invoices tell a story:

  • Who: Hauler name and license number (should be on invoice)
  • When: Date of service
  • Estimate of What: "30-yard dumpster" implies ~10 tons of debris
  • Where: Your job site address
ACTION: Create a spreadsheet:
Date Project Hauler Waste Type (Estimated) Quantity (Estimated) Invoice #
3/15/24 123 Main St Remodel ABC Waste Construction Debris 30 cu yards INV-4501

Method 2: Photo Evidence

Construction crews take photos constantly. Search your phone and crew phones for:

  • Waste containers on-site (shows type and size)
  • Hauler trucks (proves professional disposal)
  • Timestamped images (provides dates)
ACTION: Add photo evidence to your spreadsheet with notes: "Photo shows 30-yard dumpster on-site 3/15/24"

Method 3: Hauler Testimony

If you've used the same haulers for years, they may have better records than you:

  • Request all manifests for your company
  • Ask for disposal site confirmations (where waste went)
  • Get signed statements if manifests are lost (e.g., "ABC Waste removed approximately 15 tons of construction debris from 123 Main St on 3/15/24 and delivered it to XYZ Landfill")
ACTION: Email or call haulers with specific date ranges. Many will provide records for free; some charge $50-200 for research.

Method 4: Memory + Estimates

For waste where you have NO records:

  • Be honest: "Based on project scope, we estimate..."
  • Show your work: "Project involved removing 2,000 sq ft of drywall. Industry standard: 5 lbs/sq ft = ~5 tons of drywall waste."
  • Mark as estimate: Use clear labels like "Estimated based on project records"
ACTION: Document your estimation methodology. The EPA respects transparency.

Day 16-20: Organize Your Response

Comprehensive documentation organization is essential for successful EPA audit responses

Create a Narrative Report

Don't just dump a pile of documents on the EPA. Create a response package:

Cover Letter (1 page):

[Date]
[EPA Inspector Name]
[Case Number]

Re: Response to [Audit Notice Date]

Dear [Inspector Name],

[Your Company] is providing the following documentation in response to the EPA audit notice dated [Date]. We have made good-faith efforts to compile all available records for the period [Start Date] to [End Date].

Please find enclosed:
1. Waste disposal invoices (Exhibit A)
2. Hauler manifests (Exhibit B)
3. Photo documentation (Exhibit C)
4. Reconstructed records with estimation methodology (Exhibit D)

We are available for follow-up questions and committed to full compliance.

Sincerely,
[Your Name], [Title]
[Company Name]

Exhibit A: Invoices (organized by date)

  • Print or PDF all invoices
  • Highlight key info: date, waste type, quantity, hauler

Exhibit B: Manifests

  • Official manifests from haulers
  • Disposal site confirmations

Exhibit C: Photo Evidence

  • Timestamped site photos showing waste containers
  • Hauler truck photos (if available)

Exhibit D: Reconstructed Records

  • Your spreadsheet from Day 8-15
  • Clear notes: "Estimated based on invoice", "Confirmed by hauler", etc.
ACTION: Number all pages. Create a table of contents. Make it easy for the inspector to navigate.

Quality Check

Before submitting, ask yourself:

  • Did I address every item requested in the audit notice?
  • Did I clearly mark estimates vs. documented facts?
  • Did I explain gaps honestly?
  • Is my response organized and professional?
ACTION: Have someone else (business partner, attorney, compliance consultant) review your package before submission.

Day 21-25: Submit and Follow Up

How to Submit

Follow the audit notice instructions exactly:

  • Mail: Send via certified mail with return receipt
  • Email: Use the exact email address provided
  • Portal: Upload to the EPA's online system if specified
ACTION: Keep copies of everything. Save email confirmations. Track certified mail receipts.

What Happens Next

After submission, expect:

  1. Acknowledgment (1-2 weeks): EPA confirms receipt
  2. Review period (2-8 weeks): Inspector reviews your response
  3. Follow-up questions (possible): EPA may request clarification or additional records
  4. Preliminary findings (4-12 weeks): EPA outlines any violations found
  5. Settlement negotiations (variable): Discuss penalties, corrective actions
ACTION: Respond promptly to any follow-up requests. Silence looks like non-cooperation.

Day 26-30: Prevent This From Happening Again

Implement a Waste Tracking System

The best time to start tracking waste was 5 years ago. The second-best time is today.

Manual System (Free):

  • Create a project binder for each job site
  • File waste invoices immediately
  • Take photos of waste containers before hauling
  • Note dates, quantities, hauler names

Digital System ($149/year):

  • Use apps like ComplianceLogger to log waste as it happens
  • Store photos with timestamps
  • Generate audit-ready reports in minutes
ACTION: Decide on a system and implement it before the audit closes. Showing proactive change reduces penalties.

Train Your Crew

Waste compliance isn't just the owner's job:

  • Teach foremen to photograph waste containers
  • Require crew to report hazardous materials immediately
  • Make "waste tracking" part of site cleanup checklists
ACTION: Hold a 15-minute crew meeting: "Here's why waste tracking matters and how to do it."

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Ignoring the Notice

Wrong: "Maybe it'll go away."
Right: Respond within 30 days. Silence = non-cooperation = bigger fines.

2. Making Up Records

Wrong: Fabricating manifests or quantities.
Right: Be honest about estimates. The EPA can spot fake documents—and criminal penalties apply for fraud.

3. Over-Explaining or Over-Apologizing

Wrong: "We're so sorry, we're terrible at record-keeping, we know we messed up..."
Right: Professional, factual tone. "We have made good-faith efforts to compile available records."

4. Going It Alone (When You Shouldn't)

Wrong: Handling a complex audit without legal advice.
Right: If the audit involves hazardous waste, large fines, or criminal allegations, consult an environmental attorney. Most offer free consultations.

When to Get Professional Help

Consult an attorney if:

  • Audit involves hazardous waste (asbestos, lead, contaminated soil)
  • Potential fines exceed $50,000
  • Criminal violations are mentioned
  • You're overwhelmed and don't know where to start

Average cost: $2,000-10,000 for audit response assistance
Potential savings: $50,000-500,000 in reduced fines

ACTION: Call 2-3 environmental attorneys for free consultations. Ask: "What's your experience with EPA construction audits?"

Final Checklist: Are You Ready to Respond?

  • I've read the audit notice and understand what's being requested
  • I've gathered all available invoices, manifests, and photos
  • I've reconstructed missing records using honest estimates
  • I've organized my response into a professional package
  • I've reviewed my submission for completeness and accuracy
  • I'm submitting via the correct method (mail/email/portal) before the deadline
  • I've implemented a waste tracking system for future projects

The Bottom Line

An EPA audit is stressful, but it's survivable.

Most construction companies facing audits:

  • Are not fined criminally
  • Settle for fines under $25,000 (often much less)
  • Improve their processes and never face another audit

The key is prompt, honest, organized response. The EPA is not trying to destroy your business—they want to see good-faith compliance. Show them you're taking it seriously, and you'll get through this.
